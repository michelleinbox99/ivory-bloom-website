Pearl by Pearl: How a Signature Pouch Is Made

By Michelle · 5 min read

Every pouch that leaves this workroom starts the same way: one square of satin, pressed flat on the cutting table by the window.

People sometimes ask why a handmade pouch costs what a factory bag doesn't. The honest answer is time — and this is where that time goes.

The cut

Satin is unforgiving. It shows every crease, frays at a glance, and shifts under the scissors if you rush it. Each pouch body is cut on the grain from a single piece, so the sheen runs in one continuous direction — that's what makes the finished bag catch light in smooth bands instead of patchwork glints.

The gather

The signature ruffle isn't sewn on; it is the bag. A drawstring channel is stitched around the full circumference, then gathered by hand until the folds sit evenly — too loose and the pouch slumps, too tight and it puckers. Getting a bloom-like, even gather is the single fussiest step, and the one no machine does well.

The handle

Each handle is strung pearl by pearl on doubled, waxed cord, knotted internally so that even if a strand were ever cut, the pearls hold. It's the same principle as a knotted pearl necklace — jewelry construction, applied to something a flower girl will absolutely swing in circles.

The finish

Ribbon ties are heat-sealed and hand-tacked. Seams are enclosed so there's no raw edge anywhere your fingers can find. Then every pouch is stuffed, photographed in natural light, and checked against the order's color reference before it's wrapped — because "dusty rose" has to mean the same thing in your hands as it did on your screen.

Why made-to-order matters

Nothing here sits on a shelf. Your pouch is cut after you order it, in your shade, sometimes in a custom color matched to a photo of your bridesmaids' dresses. It takes about two weeks. I think the wait is part of what makes it yours.
