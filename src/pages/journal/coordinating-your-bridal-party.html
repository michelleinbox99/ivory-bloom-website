Coordinating Your Bridal Party Without Making Everything Match

By Michelle · 7 min read

The most beautiful bridal parties I've ever made pieces for weren't matched. They were related.

Somewhere along the way, "coordinated" started to mean "identical" — five women in the same dress holding the same bouquet. It photographs fine. But the weddings that look effortless, editorial, and genuinely modern follow a different principle: everyone belongs to the same story, and no one is wearing a costume.

Think palette, not color

Choose two to three hues that live near each other — dusty rose, blush, and lavender; or sage, ivory, and champagne — and let your party spread across them. Mixed-palette dresses are already popular; the trick is extending that thinking to accessories. If every bridesmaid carries the same pouch in the same color, it flattens the palette you worked to build. Instead, match each pouch to its wearer's dress, or alternate two shades through the lineup.

Repeat one element everywhere

Variation needs an anchor. Pick one detail that repeats on every person — the pearl handle, a ribbon in the same champagne tone, matching earrings — and let everything else drift within the palette. That single repeated element is what tells the eye these people are together. In my own work it's almost always the hand-strung pearl handle: six different satin shades, one unmistakable signature.

Dress the bride apart, not above

The bride shouldn't match her party — she should sit adjacent to it. If your bridesmaids carry color, carry ivory. If they wear soft florals, wear structured pearls. The contrast is what makes photographs read instantly: one figure in the composition belongs to a different register.

Don't forget the smallest members

Flower girls are where coordination gets joyfully loose. A flower girl doesn't need to match the bridesmaids — she needs to rhyme with them. A crown that borrows one flower from the bouquets, a mini pouch in the palest shade of the palette. Children read as part of the story through echo, not duplication.

A working formula

  • Two to three neighboring hues for the party
  • One repeated signature element across everyone
  • The bride adjacent — lighter, more structured, or more detailed than the party
  • Children echoing the palette at its softest

When couples order bridal party sets from the atelier, this is exactly the conversation we have — which shades, distributed how, anchored by what. It's a twenty-minute decision that shapes every photograph you'll keep.
